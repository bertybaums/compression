# Small-model CoT in restricted-vocab, narrow-domain settings

## Bottom line

Our setup — a from-scratch Reasoner trained on a ~1,000-word Up Goer Five (UGF) vocabulary with chain-of-thought traces distilled from large teachers (gpt-oss-120b, gemma-4-26b) on a narrow domain of practical philosophy — sits in a regime where published results are encouraging for **models substantially smaller than our currently-planned 1.5B**. Our current `configs/reasoner.yaml` size yields a Chinchilla ratio of 0.45 tokens per parameter at the 2M-trace target, about 45× below the 20-tokens-per-parameter compute-optimal benchmark. But the Chinchilla benchmark was derived on open-domain pretraining, and the closest precedents in our regime — TinyStories, Symbolic Chain-of-Thought Distillation, Phi-4-mini-reasoning — suggest that model sizes from **100M to 500M** are viable for coherent CoT when (a) the vocabulary is restricted, (b) the domain is narrow, and (c) the training targets are distilled from strong teachers.

## Why the question arose

Three facts about our current plan:
- The UGF vocabulary is ~3,600 word-level tokens (versus ~100K subword tokens in typical LLMs).
- Our target is "practical philosophical reasoning" — logic, critical thinking, decision theory, game theory, ethics, with narrow expansions into probability/induction and belief-justification epistemology (see `corpus/generation/generate_reasoning.py`'s `TOPICS` dict). 76 topics × 5 content types = 380 combos.
- Training traces are generated by large teacher LLMs and validated against the UGF wordlist before use. The Reasoner is a distillation student, not an open-domain learner.

Our 1.5B Reasoner will see roughly 670M corpus tokens at the 2M-trace target (calculation detail in §Chinchilla below). That is dramatically less than Chinchilla-optimal. The open question — does this suffice for competent chain-of-thought generation — motivates this scan.

## Closest analogs in the literature

### TinyStories (Eldan & Li, 2023)

The cleanest precedent for restricted-vocab small-model coherence: Microsoft researchers built a synthetic corpus whose words were drawn from the vocabulary of a typical 3–4-year-old (about 1,500 items), and showed that **models under 10M parameters — some with a single transformer block — produced fluent, grammatically consistent, multi-paragraph stories that exhibited local reasoning** (e.g., an intended cause leads to a recognized effect in the narrative). The work is noteworthy for us because it decouples two things often conflated: *linguistic competence* scales with total compute and data; *production of coherent output within a restricted distribution* does not require a large model.

TinyStories was later used as part of the pretraining mix for Phi-3, giving its design a direct line of descent into production small-model reasoning systems.

Relevance to us: our vocabulary is about 1,000 lemmas (3,600 inflected forms), similar in order of magnitude. Our domain is narrow in a different direction (pedagogical philosophical reasoning rather than children's stories), but the structural parallel is tight.

### Symbolic Chain-of-Thought Distillation (SCoTD) — Li et al., ACL 2023

Directly addresses the size-for-CoT question. The authors sampled rationalizations from a large teacher and trained students of **125M, 345M, and 1.3B parameters** on those rationalizations. Across commonsense benchmarks, SCoTD-trained students improved substantially over non-CoT baselines, and **human raters judged student chain-of-thoughts as comparable to teacher outputs** despite 2–3 orders of magnitude fewer parameters. The paper's explicit claim is that while CoT's emergence in *prompting* large models is size-dependent, CoT *production* in small models is trainable via distillation. This is the claim our project relies on.

Relevance: our gpt-oss-120b and gemma-4-26b teachers play the role of SCoTD's teacher. Our student is the Reasoner. We are doing exactly this kind of distillation, with the added twist of the UGF constraint.

### Phi-4-mini-reasoning (Microsoft, 2025)

The current production-scale realization of the pattern: **3.8B parameters, trained exclusively on ~1M synthetic math problems generated by DeepSeek-R1.** Closed domain (mathematics), synthetic-from-teacher training data, competitive with general 7B–13B reasoners on math benchmarks. The training recipe matches ours in spirit: a bounded domain, explicit CoT targets, a large teacher doing the heavy lifting. The main differences are the domain (math vs. practical philosophy) and the model size (3.8B vs. our contemplated 300M–1.5B range).

If Phi-4-mini-reasoning works at 3.8B on ~1M domain-specific distillation targets, then something analogous should work for us at comparable scale. Our 2M-target corpus and narrow-domain setup are of the same order.

### Grokked Transformers are Implicit Reasoners — Wang et al., NeurIPS 2024

Relevant for understanding generalization at small scale. The authors showed that small transformers can learn multi-step implicit reasoning on narrow algorithmic tasks, but only through *grokking*: extended training well past the point of training-set overfitting. The grokked models generalized in-distribution and, for comparison-style reasoning, out-of-distribution; for composition-style reasoning, OOD generalization was much weaker.

Relevance: our 10-epoch training regime (200K steps × 6.55B tokens of training compute on ~670M corpus tokens) sits in the extended-training zone where grokking-style generalization has been observed. Our content types include both comparison-style (argument_analysis) and composition-style (chain_of_thought) moves, so we should expect some unevenness in OOD generalization — a finding worth building into the evaluation plan.

### Scaling Laws with Vocabulary — Tao et al., NeurIPS 2024

Derives compute-optimal vocabulary sizes as a function of model size. Headline result: **"larger models deserve larger vocabularies."** Conversely, and more useful for us, smaller models pair naturally with smaller vocabularies. Our ~3,600-token vocabulary is tiny by modern LLM standards, and the paper's framework suggests it aligns best with a correspondingly smaller model. The authors find that holding FLOPs constant, loss follows a U-shape with vocabulary size, indicating an optimum rather than monotone preference. We haven't computed where our optimum falls, but the qualitative argument cuts toward a smaller Reasoner.

## How this maps onto our setup

Four properties of our project matter here, and each one pushes the "minimum viable size" down:

1. **Distillation, not emergence.** The teachers have already compressed the reasoning patterns into CoT traces. The student is not discovering CoT from scratch; it is imitating it. SCoTD is our existence-proof that 125M–1.3B models can imitate competently.

2. **Explicit CoT supervision during pretraining.** Our `chain_of_thought` content type gives explicit step-by-step targets. The Reasoner is trained directly on CoT-formatted outputs, not hoping CoT emerges from next-token prediction on arbitrary text.

3. **Restricted output space.** ~3,600 tokens means the model's next-token prediction has a fundamentally smaller entropy than a full-vocab model. Less to memorize about word co-occurrence; more parameters free for structure.

4. **Narrow domain.** 380 combos cover the range of tasks. Open-domain reasoning benchmarks (MMLU, BBH, etc.) ask much more combinatorial breadth of a model. Ours does not.

The Chinchilla ratio, derived from open-domain pretraining, does not straightforwardly apply. Phi-4-mini-reasoning is well below Chinchilla-optimal on its domain-specific run, and it works. SCoTD students at 125M on commonsense distillation data are far below Chinchilla, and they work. There is, to our knowledge, no published scaling law for "distillation on a closed narrow-vocab domain" — this is a regime where the existing laws are silent.

## Implications for the Reasoner size decision

Given the evidence above, the Reasoner does not need to be 1.5B to do CoT. The rough landscape:

| parameter count | distance from Chinchilla | CoT prognosis | rationale |
|---|---|---|---|
| 100M | ~6.7 tokens/param | Plausible but risky | TinyStories territory on vocab grounds; but our task is multi-step reasoning, not story continuation. Bert's Numerals work saw 100–300M struggle with CoT on arithmetic, which is a real data point. |
| 300M | ~2.2 tokens/param | Probably works in-distribution | Lower end of SCoTD's tested range. Distillation + explicit CoT should carry it in-distribution; OOD is the risk. |
| 500M | ~1.3 tokens/param | Should work comfortably | Sweet spot. Above SCoTD's low end; well above TinyStories; below Phi-mini-reasoning. Trains ~3× faster than 1.5B on the same hardware. |
| 1B | ~0.67 tokens/param | Works, mildly undertrained | Margin of safety. |
| 1.5B (current plan) | ~0.45 tokens/param | Works, heavily undertrained | Significant over-parameterization; memorization risk past epoch 4 or so. |

The Chinchilla-gap column matters less than it looks in this regime, but it is not zero. Heavy over-parameterization trains a model that "could have learned more" if only it had more data — and with ~10 epochs of repeated exposure, some fraction of the 1.5B's capacity is memorizing specific traces rather than abstracting patterns.

### A paper-friendly recommendation

Rather than pick one size, **train three sizes in parallel and report the scaling curve.** This would answer a question the literature has not yet answered: *at what parameter count does UGF-constrained philosophical CoT emerge?* The setup is unusual enough (1,000-word vocabulary, narrow domain, full distillation) that the answer is not obvious from prior work. If coherent CoT emerges at 200M and flattens by 500M, the headline result is that vocabulary compression reduces the capacity floor for reasoning — a genuinely interesting claim. If it requires 1B+, we learn something too.

A practical version: train **200M, 500M, and 1.5B** with the same corpus and training budget, evaluate held-out reasoning-trace perplexity and (if feasible) LLM-judged coherence on held-out topics. The 200M run costs a few GPU-hours on an A6000; the 500M run costs ~1–2 days; the 1.5B run costs 3–5 days. Combined cost is less than double the 1.5B-only plan, and the paper result is meaningfully stronger.

### Where Numerals warns us

Bert's Numerals work is a relevant counterweight: tiny transformers (13K–52K params) failed to learn chain-of-thought for arithmetic even with explicit carry-level scaffolding, reaching only 14–92% accuracy across scaffold formats after 80K–160K steps. That is 4–5 orders of magnitude smaller than the 100M we are contemplating, on a simpler task. The direct lesson is that there is a floor below which CoT does not learn; the indirect lesson is that scaffolding and explicit structure only help down to that floor. Our 100M–200M range is probably well above Numerals' observed floor, but we should take empirical risk seriously and not skip the 500M variant if we include a 200M one.

## Open questions worth resolving empirically

- At what Reasoner size does held-out UGF CoT perplexity plateau? This localizes the emergence threshold.
- How does OOD topic generalization degrade as we shrink the model? Grokking work suggests comparison-style reasoning survives better than composition-style; we have both in our content types.
- Does the parallel-corpus UGF side (229K additional passages, now in the training mix) meaningfully improve reasoning-trace quality, or is it incidental? An ablation would clarify.
- How does SFT on a focus area (e.g., counterexample analysis, conditional reasoning) behave when the pretrained base is 200M vs 1.5B? Catastrophic forgetting resistance is easier at larger scales, but maybe narrow-vocab mitigates this.
