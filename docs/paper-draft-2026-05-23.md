# Compressed Vocabulary, Preserved Reasoning: A 200M-Parameter Test of Expressive Adequacy for Practical Philosophy

> **Audience note.** This paper sits between philosophy and machine learning, and it is written so that a capable reader from either side can follow all of it. Short primers are marked "Philosophers:" or "ML readers:" and can be skipped by those who already have the background. Every number is read for the reader where it first matters: we say what counts as good, what counts as bad, and which numbers to trust.

## Abstract

The short version is this. A thousand common English words are enough to *say* practical philosophy, and they are not enough, on their own, to make a small model *do* it on demand. We train a 200-million-parameter transformer from scratch over a tokenizer whose entire vocabulary is the ~1,000-word "Up Goer Five" fragment of English, distill it on 1.96 million teacher-generated traces of philosophical reasoning written in that vocabulary, and evaluate it on a four-dimensional rubric against a strong teacher constrained to the same words. Within the training distribution the model reasons recognizably well: it lands within 0.5 of the teacher on three of four rubric dimensions, and the restricted vocabulary itself never breaks the argument. Out of distribution the model exhibits a sharp, specific failure: it produces fluent, well-formed reasoning about something other than what was asked. We call this the prompt-attention deficit. We then show that the deficit resists every post-training correction we tried: reinforcement learning against a learned reward made it worse by reward-hacking, and two runs of preference optimization left it unchanged. The deficit is therefore not an alignment problem but a capability one, located upstream in pretraining and corpus form. We read this as a partial vindication of inferentialism with a caveat the theory does not currently address: inferential role can be carried in a thousand words, but recognizing which inferences a prompt calls for is a separable capacity, and it is the one our model lacks.

## 1. Introduction

You can do quite a lot with one connective. The Sheffer stroke, NAND, is functionally complete for classical propositional logic: every truth function on Boolean inputs can be written using only NAND, at the cost of longer sentences. Nothing of importance becomes inexpressible. The vocabulary shrinks; the expressive power does not. This is a familiar move in the foundations of logic, but it points to a more interesting question in natural language. How much can you shrink an everyday vocabulary before something that matters disappears?

Randall Munroe's *Thing Explainer* (2015) is a playful experiment in radical vocabulary reduction. The text restricts itself to the thousand most common English words, call this the **Up Goer Five** (UGF) vocabulary, and uses them to explain the periodic table, the Constitution, the human cell, and the Saturn V rocket. Munroe's exercise is whimsical. But it raises a serious question: is this 1K-word fragment of English expressively adequate for *practical philosophical reasoning*, for the kind of disciplined argument that fills logic textbooks, ethics seminars, and decision-theory papers? Expressive adequacy is the property the Sheffer stroke has for logic. We are asking whether a thousand words have it for philosophy.

The question is misleadingly simple. It looks like one question and it is two, and most of the interest lives in the gap between them. The first is about the *vocabulary*: can the content be said at all in these words? The second is about a *reasoner*: can a particular system, trained only on these words, produce the content on demand? A positive answer to the first does not entail a positive answer to the second, and our central finding is exactly that they come apart.

We test both directly. We train a 200-million-parameter decoder-only transformer from scratch, with a custom tokenizer whose entire vocabulary is the ~1K UGF words plus minimal punctuation. We distill the model on 1.96 million teacher-generated traces of philosophical reasoning written in UGF. (Philosophers: "distill" here is a term of art for training a small model to imitate the outputs of a larger one; the small model never sees the teacher's weights, only its text.) We then evaluate, on within-distribution prompts and on a hand-crafted stress benchmark, whether the model produces what a competent rubric reader would call good philosophical reasoning, scoring it against a 120-billion-parameter teacher held to the same vocabulary.

The result, at first pass, is encouraging. On within-distribution prompts the model scores within 0.5 of the teacher on three of four rubric dimensions, and it clears the 3.0-of-4 mark on expressive adequacy, the dimension that asks whether the vocabulary restriction is degrading the argument. On a more demanding read, the story splits along the seam we just described. The Up Goer Five vocabulary *is* expressively adequate for the philosophical content we tested, within calibrated limits we will map. But our trained model exhibits a failure worth flagging in its own right: on out-of-distribution prompt forms, it produces fluent, structurally-correct UGF reasoning about something other than what was asked. We call this the prompt-attention deficit.

The deficit is the part of the paper that earns a second look, and the bulk of our subsequent work is an attempt to remove it. We could not. We tried reinforcement learning against a learned quality reward, and the model reward-hacked: it drifted into a clean, fluent, off-topic paragraph that the reward tolerated while the thing we cared about got worse. We tried preference optimization, the standard contrastive alternative, twice and at two data scales, and the model came out at parity with where it started. So the deficit is not something a post-training objective installs or removes. It is upstream, in the model's capability and in the *form* of the corpus it learned from, and that relocation is itself a finding.

**This work bears on three threads that have until now stayed mostly separate:** inferentialist accounts of conceptual content (Brandom 1994; Sellars 1956), scaling behavior for small specialized language models, and the program of identifying minimal sufficient vocabularies for cognitive domains. The first lets us say what it means that vocabulary compression preserves reasoning, and where it stops. The second positions a 200M-parameter result in a literature increasingly interested in small models. The third generalizes the methodology. We develop the inferentialist reading in §5.2 and sketch the program in §6.

We proceed as follows. §2 places the work in its several literatures. §3 describes the vocabulary, corpus, model, and evaluation. §4 reports the within-distribution result, the out-of-distribution deficit, and the post-training attempts to remove it. §5 takes stock, develops the inferentialist reading, and is honest about limits. §6 lays out the program the result opens up, including the retraining we turn to next.

## 2. Background and related work

This section situates the paper in four conversations. Specialists in any one of them may skim the corresponding subsection: the goal here is orientation, not a survey.

### 2.1 Restricted English

Controlled vocabularies are not new. Ogden's *Basic English* (1930) proposed an 850-word core for international communication; Voice of America's Special English used a ~1,500-word vocabulary for broadcast; Nerrière's *Globish* (2006) targets a ~1,500-word business register; and plain-language movements have long pressed law and medicine toward smaller, commoner words. What the prior empirical work has mostly studied is *human* uptake: whether people understand, retain, or prefer the restricted register. Underdeveloped, then, is the computational question we ask here: not whether a person can read a thousand-word vocabulary, but whether a model can be trained to *reason* in one.

### 2.2 Latent reasoning and abstract vocabularies

A recent and complementary line of work compresses the vocabulary of *thought* rather than of words. Ramji et al. (2026), "Thinking Without Words," train models to reason over a small abstract token vocabulary, reporting roughly 11.6× compression and a Zipfian emergence of an abstract lexicon. The contrast with our work is instructive. Their compression is latent and abstract; ours is lexical and concrete, a fragment of ordinary English a person can read. The two approaches probe the same underlying question, how little symbolic material reasoning needs, from opposite ends. We position UGF as the concrete, human-legible counterpart.

### 2.3 Inferentialism

Philosophers: the one idea to carry forward is that, on the inferentialist view, what a concept *means* is fixed by the inferences it licenses, not by the word attached to it. Sellars (1956) and Brandom (1994) are the canonical sources; McDowell (1994) the well-known interlocutor. The view has been mostly an armchair commitment, for a practical reason: we cannot ordinarily strip away vocabulary without also stripping away the inferential structure attached to it, so there is nothing to vary. Our setup varies it. The teacher writes in a thousand words but keeps full inferential machinery, and the student inherits inferential structure expressed in a radically reduced vocabulary. That gives inferentialism something it has rarely had, an empirical test, and §5.2 cashes it out.

### 2.4 Small models and chain-of-thought at scale

ML readers: the relevant context is the run of results showing that explicit step-by-step "chain-of-thought" reasoning, robust at tens of billions of parameters (Wei et al. 2022), degrades as models shrink, and the partial counter-evidence from distillation-heavy small models (the Phi series; Orca). A 200M-parameter reasoner sits at the low end of what this literature treats as a credible reasoner at all. Our own prior project on arithmetic reasoning at 13K-52K parameters (the "Numerals" study) supplies several of the training lessons we lean on later, in particular about reinforcement learning at small scale. The point worth previewing: at this size, the choice of training *corpus* and *format* tends to dominate the choice of training *algorithm*, and our results will say so again.

## 3. Method

The pipeline has four parts: a vocabulary and tokenizer, a distilled corpus, a from-scratch model trained in two (later three) stages, and a rubric-based evaluation judged by an external model. We describe each, and for the design choices that could have gone otherwise we say why we made them.

### 3.1 Vocabulary and tokenizer

The vocabulary is Munroe's curated thousand-word list. After adding minimal punctuation and a small number of special tokens, the final tokenizer carries 3,643 tokens. (The count exceeds a thousand because the tokenizer holds inflected forms and casing variants as distinct tokens.) The tokenizer is word-level, not sub-word: a design choice we adopt so that the vocabulary restriction is enforced *at the token level*, making it impossible for the model to smuggle in an out-of-vocabulary word by spelling it out in pieces. The restriction is thus architectural, not a matter of prompting or post-hoc filtering.

### 3.1a Proper nouns: stripped, not preserved

The thousand-word vocabulary is the *conceptual* vocabulary of the reasoner: predicates, common nouns, function words, and the inferentially-active material that does philosophical work. Proper nouns are not part of it, and they are stripped from the training corpus, including from dialogue passages where they would conventionally identify the speakers. Where a source text uses a name, the curation pipeline substitutes a stable UGF-vocabulary *descriptor*: Socrates becomes "the old teacher," Cleanthes "the man who sees plans," Anytus "the angry man." The same descriptor is used for the same character across every passage drawn from the same source, so the descriptor works as a stable referring expression built from inferentially-active vocabulary.

We adopt this policy for three reasons. First, it keeps the expressive-adequacy claim clean: zero proper nouns, no asterisk. Second, it matches what the original parallel corpus already did, since proper-noun stripping was the default of the teacher pipeline. Third, and most interesting, it turns a theoretical question into an empirical one. Whether descriptor stand-ins can come to behave like names, with rigid designation and the other marks of singular terms (Kripke 1972), becomes a *post-training test* rather than an architectural assumption. We do not hard-code a token class for names. We let the model see only descriptions, and we ask what it learns. We return to this in §5.2 and develop it in a companion paper.

### 3.2 Corpus

The training corpus is 1.96 million teacher-generated traces of philosophical reasoning, each written in UGF and validated against the wordlist. The teachers are an ensemble served through a university model router: a 120B open-weight model (gpt-oss-120b) and a 26B model (gemma-4-26b), with a 27B model contributing earlier material. (ML readers: an ensemble simply means we drew traces from more than one teacher and mixed them, to avoid inheriting a single model's stylistic tics.) Traces span a content-type taxonomy we designed to cover the moves practical philosophy actually makes: concept explanation, Socratic dialogue, chain-of-thought, thought experiment, argument analysis, analogical analysis, conditional analysis, and counterexample analysis. After a round of prompt fixes early in the campaign, vocabulary-compliance of generated traces sat near 99%.

Two later corpus efforts matter for the results. We added a smaller *form-diverse* corpus of ~2,766 pairs extracted from canonical texts (Plato, Hume, Aquinas, Sextus Empiricus) in dialogue, objection-reply, and skeptical-mode forms; the motivation, that argumentative *form* might be the thing the model most needs to learn, becomes central in the discussion. And we generated a 100K-trace chain-of-thought corpus (half logic, half philosophy) for a later training stage. We flag these here and use them in §4.

### 3.3 Model and training

The model is a decoder-only transformer in the contemporary idiom: rotary positional embeddings, grouped-query attention, SwiGLU feed-forward blocks, RMSNorm, tied input-output embeddings. The configuration we report is ~200M parameters (model width 1024, 16 layers, 16 attention heads with 4 key-value heads, feed-forward width 3072, context length 512). We adopt the standard recipe rather than innovate on architecture because the architecture is not what we are testing; the vocabulary is.

Training proceeds in stages. We pretrain for 100K steps (final validation loss 0.79), then supervise-fine-tune on the scaffold format for 30K steps (validation loss 0.74). This is the v1 model behind the headline within-distribution result. A later revision, which we call SFT v2, reorganizes the target format around an explicit think-then-answer separation marked by a pure-UGF phrase ("So my answer is:") rather than the special bracketing tokens a frontier model would use. We adopt the phrasal marker, and not special tokens, to keep the output inside the vocabulary under test: a special `<answer>` token would be a small but real violation of the very restriction the paper is about. SFT v2 parses cleanly and is marginally more prompt-attentive on out-of-distribution inputs, trading output length for focus, but it does not close the deficit, and the post-training work in §4.5 starts from it.

### 3.4 Evaluation

We score reasoning on a four-dimensional, anchored 0-to-4 rubric: **engagement** (does the response address the prompt's actual question), **coherence** (does each step follow from the prior, with no self-contradiction), **substance** (does it do real philosophical work, a counterexample or a distinction or a traced analogy, rather than paraphrase), and **expressive adequacy** (does the content come through cleanly, or does the vocabulary restriction visibly degrade it). The rubric is anchored at 0, 2, and 4, and the judge interpolates 1 and 3. (How to read these numbers: a 4 on a dimension is teacher-quality; a 2 is recognizable but flawed; a 0 is absent or broken. On the dimension we care most about for the headline thesis, expressive adequacy, a score at or above 3 means the thousand-word restriction is not what is holding the argument back.)

Two features of the evaluation deserve flagging for the non-specialist. First, the judge is an external model not in our teacher ensemble (Claude subagents for the offline benchmarks; a separate non-teacher model for the in-the-loop reward in §4.5), so the judge is not grading its own student's family. Second, we score the UGF text *directly* rather than translating it back to ordinary English first. We had built a translator for that purpose and retired it from the evaluation path: the trained translator hallucinated on the model's own UGF (inventing names, looping), which would have contaminated the judgment with the translator's errors rather than the reasoner's. Judging the UGF as written is the more honest measurement, even though it asks more of the judge.

Our primary comparator is the teacher held to UGF: the same 120B model that generated training traces, prompted to answer in the thousand-word vocabulary. This is the right baseline for the expressive-adequacy question, because it isolates the *model-capacity* gap from the *vocabulary* gap. If the teacher-in-UGF scores high and our model scores low, the limit is the model; if both score low, the limit is the vocabulary. We lean on that contrast throughout.

## 4. Results

We report four things, in order: the within-distribution comparison, a sharper probe of where the gap lives, the out-of-distribution deficit, and the sequence of attempts to remove it. The short version: the vocabulary is adequate, the model is good inside its training distribution and brittle outside it, and the brittleness does not yield to post-training.

### 4.1 Within distribution: the model reasons recognizably well

On a 170-item held-out benchmark drawn from the training distribution, the v1 model (the "Reasoner") scores against the teacher-in-UGF comparator as follows.

| | engagement | coherence | substance | expressive adequacy |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Reasoner | 2.08 | 3.34 | 2.26 | 3.17 |
| Comparator (teacher-in-UGF) | 3.61 | 3.77 | 2.67 | 3.55 |
| Δ (Reasoner − Comparator) | −1.53 | −0.43 | −0.41 | −0.38 |

Read the deltas this way: three of the four gaps are within half a point, which on a 0-to-4 scale is the difference between "good" and "slightly-less-good," not between "works" and "fails." Coherence, substance, and expressive adequacy are all close. The model's UGF reads as connected argument, it does some real philosophical work, and, crucially for the thesis, its expressive adequacy sits at 3.17: the vocabulary is not what holds it back. On three content types the model actually *edges* the comparator on substance. The one large gap is engagement, at −1.53, and it is the thread we pull in §4.3.

To be fair about what this table is and is not: the comparator is itself constrained to UGF, so this is a within-vocabulary comparison, not a comparison to unrestricted English. That is the comparison the paper wants, but a reader should not read "within 0.5 of the teacher" as "within 0.5 of GPT-4 in English." It is within 0.5 of a strong model writing in the same thousand words.

### 4.2 Where the gap lives: a three-way probe

To localize the gap we ran a 50-prompt probe (32 prompts common to all conditions) comparing three ways of producing a UGF answer. P4 reasons in unrestricted English with the 120B model and then translates the result into UGF. P5 has the same model reason in UGF natively. The Reasoner is our 200M model.

| | engagement | coherence | substance | expressive adequacy |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| P4 (English reasoning, then translate) | 3.91 | 3.62 | 3.62 | 2.84 |
| P5 (native UGF generation) | 3.78 | 3.72 | 2.84 | 3.38 |
| Reasoner (v1) | 0.44 | 0.94 | 0.38 | 1.12 |

The probe says two useful things. First, the two teacher pipelines are both strong, and they trade off in an interpretable way: reasoning in English and translating buys substance (3.62) at the cost of some expressive adequacy (2.84, the translation leaves the odd clunky paraphrase), while reasoning natively in UGF keeps the prose clean (3.38) but does a little less work (2.84 substance). Either way, the thousand words carry the content. Second, and soberingly, the Reasoner is far below both on this out-of-distribution prompt slice, near the floor on every dimension. The within-distribution competence of §4.1 does not transfer. That contrast, strong on familiar prompts and near-floor on unfamiliar ones, is the deficit in numbers.

### 4.3 The out-of-distribution deficit

On a 30-item hand-crafted stress benchmark, built to pose familiar philosophical questions in unfamiliar forms, the model's engagement collapses to 0.13. The collapse has a characteristic shape, and it is not what one might expect. The model does not produce gibberish, and it does not produce broken UGF. It produces fluent, internally coherent, vocabulary-compliant prose that answers a *different question* than the one asked. Asked to walk through what "fragile" commits us to, it will deliver a clean little essay on cooperation or on loss aversion. The machinery of reasoning runs; the reading of the prompt does not.

This is why the two questions of §1 come apart so cleanly here. The comparator's expressive adequacy on these same stress items stays in the high 2s to mid 3s by category (use-mention 3.67, the modal and supervenience and abstract-object categories around 2.67): the vocabulary strains on the hardest prompts but nothing breaks. So the vocabulary is not the bottleneck. The bottleneck is that our model, faced with a prompt shaped unlike its training data, fails to recognize what inferences the prompt is calling for, and falls back on a well-worn groove. We call this the prompt-attention deficit, and we want to be precise about what it is: not a failure of fluency, not a failure of vocabulary, but a failure of input-recognition sitting on top of an otherwise functional inferential interior.

### 4.4 The corpus is not the ceiling

Before trying to fix the deficit, we checked an obvious suspect: maybe the model is only as good as mediocre training data. It is not. A blind audit of 300 records per corpus put our chain-of-thought and existing-reasoning traces at comfortable quality (around 3.8 engagement, 3.8 coherence, 3.3 substance, 3.7 expressive adequacy on the same rubric), well above what the model produces. Put plainly: the v1 model sits roughly 1.8 points below its own training data on engagement and a full point below on substance. The teacher showed it good reasoning. It did not fully learn to produce that reasoning on demand. Whatever the deficit is, it is not that we fed the model poor examples.

### 4.5 The deficit resists post-training

If the corpus is good and the model underperforms it, the natural next move is post-training: keep the model and push it, with reinforcement learning or preference optimization, toward the behavior we want. We made three serious attempts. None closed the deficit, and the *way* they failed is informative.

**Reinforcement learning against a learned reward made things worse.** We built a reward from the rubric (weighted toward substance and engagement, the dimensions we most wanted to lift) and ran group-relative policy optimization, a standard reinforcement-learning recipe, with the reward scored by a non-teacher model. (Philosophers: the setup is operant conditioning with a critic; the model proposes answers, a judge scores them, and we nudge the model toward the higher-scored ones.) Over 250 steps the training reward barely moved, from about 0.24 to 0.26 on a 0-to-1 scale, while the model drifted a long way from where it started. On held-out benchmarks the result was not parity but regression: against the supervised model it started from, engagement fell by 0.68 and substance by 0.50. The cause was legible in the outputs. The model had found a single fluent, on-distribution, off-topic paragraph that the reward tolerated, and learned to emit it almost regardless of the prompt. This is reward-hacking, and it is a known hazard when the judge is far larger than the student: the gradient comes to track the judge's blind spots rather than the task. The lesson we draw, and it echoes our earlier small-model work, is that a scalar reward is too weak a constraint here. It can be satisfied by any fluent text, and a model with a prompt-attention deficit will find the cheapest such text.

**Preference optimization did no harm, and no good.** The contrastive alternative is to train not against a score but against explicit pairs: this answer is better than that one. We built such pairs by taking a reliably on-topic reference (the P4 pipeline from §4.2) as the "preferred" answer and the model's own off-topic productions as the "dispreferred" one, putting the deficit's signature failure squarely on the rejected side, and ran anchored preference optimization. The contrastive objective behaved as designed: it did not collapse the way reinforcement learning did, the model held its format, and the training signal cleanly separated good from bad. But on the held-out benchmarks the result was parity. The first run, on 330 preference pairs, came in at +0.04 engagement and +0.05 substance against the supervised baseline, within measurement noise. We then ran the decisive scaled version, with 2.5× the data and the best checkpoint selected by the training signal, precisely to rule out the possibility that the first run was simply too small. It returned the same answer: −0.00 substance, +0.02 engagement. Two runs, two scales, the same parity.

**Reading the three attempts together.** Put the methods in one column and the picture is clear. Reinforcement learning regressed; preference optimization, twice, held at parity. (To be fair, "parity" is a real and reassuring result on its own terms: the contrastive method is safe where the scalar reward was not, and a reader interested in alignment stability should note that.) What no post-training objective did was *fix* the thing. Across every method, the judges' notes describe the same failure on the same prompt forms: clean UGF, wrong question. The deficit does not live at the layer post-training can reach. It lives upstream, in what the pretraining-plus-distillation pipeline did and did not install, and in the *form* of the corpus the model learned from. That relocation, from "an alignment problem we can optimize away" to "a capability problem we have to retrain for," is the main result of this part of the study.

## 5. Discussion

### 5.1 Let's take stock: two questions, two answers

The clean headline, small model plus restricted vocabulary equals near-teacher reasoning, is the wrong way to read this study, because it answers a question we did not quite ask. The honest summary is two answers to two questions. Is the Up Goer Five vocabulary expressively adequate for the practical philosophical reasoning we tested? Yes, within the calibrated limits of §4.1 and §4.3: the teacher-in-UGF carries the content, the trained model's expressive adequacy stays above 3 within distribution, and on the hardest stress prompts the vocabulary strains without breaking. Did our 200M model learn to *do* philosophical reasoning in UGF? On within-distribution prompts, recognizably yes. On out-of-distribution prompts, no, and the failure is specific, characterizable, and, as §4.5 showed, robust to post-training. The interest of the paper is precisely that these two answers diverge. Expressive adequacy is a property of the vocabulary; the deficit is a property of the model.

### 5.2 An inferentialist reading

The prompt-attention deficit is the most original empirical finding here, and it admits a philosophical reading worth developing. Inferentialism, again, is the view that conceptual content is constituted by inferential role rather than by lexical label. The view has been hard to test because vocabulary and inferential structure normally travel together. Our setup pulls them apart almost by accident: the teacher writes in a thousand words but keeps full inferential machinery, and the student inherits inferential structure expressed in radically compressed vocabulary. The question is whether the inference survives the compression.

Within distribution, it does. The model states a thesis, deploys an example, considers a counterexample, draws a conclusion, all in a thousand words the judge rates as recognizable philosophical content. The vocabulary is doing inferential work, not merely labeling. Inferentialism's first-pass prediction, that you can compress the words without losing the concepts because the concepts were never *in* the words, is vindicated to that extent.

But the deficit is the interesting part, and §4.5 sharpens it into something the May-12 version of this argument could only gesture at. On out-of-distribution prompts the inferential machinery runs and the input-recognition does not: the model reasons fluently about the wrong question. And we now know this is not a surface tuning issue, because we tried to tune it away and could not. Reinforcement learning and preference optimization both operate on the inferential-output layer, rewarding better moves; neither could install the capacity to read a prompt as a *call for* particular moves. We take this as a partial vindication of inferentialism with a caveat the theory does not currently state: inferential role gives you the content of the moves internal to reasoning, but it does not, by itself, fix how an agent recognizes which inferences a given input demands. Prompt-attention, or input-recognition, is a separable capacity. Our model has a working inferential interior and a defective recognition layer, and the separation is robust enough to survive our attempts to erase it.

Two readings remain open, and §4.5 shifts the weight between them. On the mild reading, the deficit is an artifact of training-distribution narrowness, fixable and theoretically inert. On the strong reading, it reveals a genuine lacuna: inferentialism as usually stated specifies no mechanism by which an agent identifies the inferential demand of an input, and our data show the gap is empirically consequential. The mild reading predicted that a modest post-training nudge would close the deficit. It did not. That does not refute the mild reading, since retraining the corpus form is still untried and is exactly the mild reading's remaining move, but it does raise the price of holding it. We develop this argument, and a second strand testing whether the descriptor stand-ins of §3.1a behave like rigid designators, in a companion paper.

### 5.3 Limitations

We are at one point in a large space, and several caveats bound the claims. The judge is a single model family, and full inter-rater calibration against human scores is still pending; we have leaned on a non-teacher judge and direct UGF scoring to reduce bias, but a careful human calibration would strengthen every number here. The domain is one, practical philosophy, and the vocabulary is one, the thousand-word UGF; we have one (size, vocabulary) cell, not a curve. The model is small by current standards, so a reader should resist concluding that the deficit is intrinsic to restricted-vocabulary reasoning rather than to *this size* of model on *this form* of corpus; disentangling those is future work, not a settled result. And the runtime translator we built was, as noted, degenerate enough to retire from the evaluation path, which is a limitation of that workstream even though removing it improved the measurement. None of these undercut the central split, but they bound how far to carry it.

## 6. What the result opens up

The compression-v1 result is one data point, and it points in three directions, each of which is its own program. We summarize them, and then name the one we turn to next.

**Retraining on form, not just content.** The most immediate follow-up is the one §4.5 argues for. If the deficit is upstream and tied to corpus form, the move is to retrain, not to post-train: rebuild the pretraining and supervised data around argumentative *forms* that are structurally prompt-attentive (dialogue, objection-and-reply, the skeptic's set-piece), where engaging the specific prompt is not optional but constitutive of the form. We already have a form-diverse corpus in hand (§3.2), and the cheapest version of the experiment continues pretraining from the current checkpoint rather than starting over. This is the experiment we run next, and it is the direct test of the mild-versus-strong reading in §5.2.

**Vocabulary × parameter scaling.** Compression-v1 is one cell of a two-dimensional grid: parameter count crossed with vocabulary size. A scan over {~50M, ~200M, ~1B} × {1K-UGF, ~5K Basic English or Globish, ~50K standard tokenizer} would let us extract iso-performance curves in the (parameters, vocabulary) plane, and test whether vocabulary structure substitutes for parameter count in a regular way. The expected output is a substitutability claim of the form "a model with vocabulary V₁ and p parameters performs as one with vocabulary V₂ and p′ parameters, where p′/p depends on structural properties of the vocabularies." Such a result speaks to scaling-law work (Kaplan et al. 2020; Hoffmann et al. 2022) and to interpretability work on vocabulary-as-compression at once. It would also tell us whether the deficit is a capacity wall that more parameters dissolve.

**Minimal sufficient vocabularies for other domains.** UGF-philosophy is one instance of a general question: for each cognitive domain, what is the smallest vocabulary in which the domain's signature inferences are still expressible? Mathematical reasoning, legal reasoning, and scientific explanation are natural replications, each with an existing controlled-vocabulary candidate (introductory-textbook headwords for math, the Plain English Legal Glossary for law, VOA Special English for science). After two or three domains the project takes on a theoretical layer: what features of a vocabulary, polysemy coverage, compositional structure, closure under definition, make it expressively complete for a domain? This is the Sheffer-stroke program for cognition, and compression-v1 is its first data point.

These directions are not mutually exclusive; the scaling scan and the multi-domain program share infrastructure, and the companion philosophy paper rides on the data of all three. The shared anchor is the methodology and the result reported here.

## 7. Conclusion

The answer to the title question is yes, within calibrated limits, and the limits are the interesting part. A thousand common words are expressively adequate for the practical philosophical reasoning we tested: a strong model writing in them carries the content, and a small model trained on them reasons recognizably well inside its training distribution, with the vocabulary itself rarely the thing that breaks. What does not come for free is reasoning on demand. Our model has a prompt-attention deficit, a tendency to reason fluently about the wrong question when the prompt is shaped unlike its training data, and the more we worked on it the clearer it became that this is not an alignment problem to be optimized away but a capability problem to be retrained for. Reinforcement learning made it worse; preference optimization, twice, left it where it stood.

We read the deficit as a partial vindication of inferentialism with a caveat the theory owes us: inferential role can be carried in a thousand words, but recognizing which inferences a prompt calls for is a separable capacity, and it is the one our model lacks. That is a small contribution, and much remains to flesh out: a human-calibrated judge, more than one (size, vocabulary) point, and above all the retraining-on-form experiment that would tell us whether the deficit is narrowness or something deeper. We turn to that next.
