## Thesis

Brandom's inferentialism (and behind it, Sellars's) holds that conceptual content is constituted by inferential role rather than by lexical labels. The claim has been mostly defended on conceptual grounds — there has been very little empirical purchase. Compression-v1 provides one: when ~95% of a language's vocabulary is stripped away but the inferential connections in its remaining 5% are preserved, does the reasoning capacity survive?

The compression-v1 data say *partially*. On within-distribution prompts, reasoning survives — the 1K-word model produces inferentially-cogent philosophical content. On out-of-distribution prompts, a specific failure mode appears: the *form* of inference is preserved but the model fails to *recognize* the prompt as triggering the relevant inferences. We call this the **prompt-attention deficit**.

We argue this is exactly what a more careful reading of inferentialism predicts: inferential role is preserved under vocabulary compression, but the input-recognition capacity — the ability to read a prompt as making a particular inferential demand — is a separate, learnable layer that inferentialism does not itself fix. The compression-v1 data thus provide both a partial vindication and a frontier where the theory needs supplementation.

## Argument structure (proposed)

1. **Background on inferentialism.** Sellars 1956 ("Empiricism and the Philosophy of Mind"); Brandom 1994 (*Making It Explicit*) and 2000 (*Articulating Reasons*); McDowell 1994 (*Mind and World*). Key claim: a concept's content is its position in a network of inferential commitments and entitlements; lexical labels are inessential.

2. **Empirical underdetermination of inferentialism.** Most existing arguments are armchair; the theory has been hard to test because we cannot easily strip away vocabulary without also stripping away inferential structure. Compression-v1 offers a vocabulary-controlled experiment: hold the inferential connections fixed (via teacher distillation from a fluent reasoner), strip the vocabulary to 1K words, see what survives.

3. **The compression-v1 setup as test apparatus.** Brief description: 200M-parameter model, 1K-word tokenizer, teacher-distilled corpus, four-dimension rubric eval. The key methodological move: the corpus is generated by competent English-speaking teachers writing *in UGF* — they retain full inferential machinery and constrain only their output vocabulary. The student model thus inherits inferential structure expressed in compressed vocabulary.

4. **The within-distribution result.** On 170 holdout items, the model scores within 0.5 of the 120B teacher on three of four rubric dimensions, and ≥3.0 (out of 4) on expressive_adequacy across all content types. The inferential moves are visibly present in the output; the philosophical content is recoverable. **First-order claim:** inferential role survives vocabulary compression.

5. **The out-of-distribution result.** On 30 stress-bench items whose prompt form differs from the corpus distribution, the model's engagement collapses to ~0.13 while coherence and expressive_adequacy stay relatively intact (~2.0+). The model produces fluent, inferentially-structured UGF *about something other than the prompt's question*. The inferential machinery runs in a vacuum.

6. **Reading this through inferentialism.** Two interpretations:
   - **Mild reading:** the prompt-attention deficit is an engineering artifact of training-distribution narrowness, irrelevant to inferentialism per se. Future engineering work could fix it without bearing on the theory.
   - **Stronger reading:** the deficit reveals a *gap in inferentialism as usually stated*. Inferential role gives you the content of the moves *internal* to reasoning. It does not, by itself, fix how an agent reads a prompt as a *call for* particular inferences. Prompt-attention is a separate cognitive capacity — perhaps Sellars's "perceptual report" function, perhaps something not previously cataloged. The model has a fully functional inferential interior with a defective input-recognition layer.

   The stronger reading is the more interesting paper. It positions compression-v1 not as evidence *for* or *against* inferentialism, but as a probe that distinguishes inferentialism's strong claims from its silent assumptions.

7. **Implications and open questions.**
   - Inferentialism needs an articulated theory of input-recognition. Some candidate moves: Brandom's pragmatist concept of "scorekeeping" already implies it but does not develop it; McDowell's "openness to the world" gestures at it. The compression-v1 finding suggests this lacuna is empirically consequential.
   - The mild and strong readings make different predictions about thread B (engineering fixes). If a second SFT pass on diverse prompt forms closes the deficit fully without otherwise changing the model, the mild reading wins. If the deficit is robust to engineering, the strong reading wins. *Thread B is therefore also a philosophical experiment.*
   - The compression-v1 methodology generalizes (see `sheffer.md`) — each domain provides another inferentialist test.

8. **Conclusion.** Compression-v1 is the first empirical vocabulary-stripping test of inferentialism. The result vindicates inferentialism's core claim on within-distribution data and identifies a specific frontier (input-recognition) where the theory needs supplementation. The frontier is itself testable.

## Cross-link: Plato as test apparatus (added May 12 2026)

The form-diverse retraining proposal (`docs/followups/corpus-diversification.md`) would materially strengthen this paper. Socratic dialogue is *literally* inferentialism caught in the act — the interlocutor's whole job is to track the inferential commitments of the previous speech act. A Plato-trained Reasoner is therefore not just a model that happens to do philosophy; it is an instantiation of Brandom's "scorekeeping" idea. If the corpus-diversification experiment runs and the form-diverse Reasoner closes the prompt-attention deficit, the inferentialist reading gains a uniquely clean piece of evidence: scorekeeping-as-training-data produces scorekeeping-capable models, which is a substantive prediction inferentialism arguably should make and has not been in a position to test.

If the corpus-diversification experiment runs and the deficit *persists*, that is also informative — it suggests that scorekeeping is not reducible to exposure-to-scorekeeping-examples, which would be evidence against an overly behaviorist reading of inferentialism. Either way, the experiment sharpens the philosophical argument.

The companion ML paper (G) need not commit to either outcome; this philosophy-side paper can be written after the corpus experiment lands and incorporate whichever result obtains.

## Second pillar: singular-term emergence under strict name-stripping (added May 12 2026)

The corpus-diversification campaign operates under Policy A: all proper nouns are stripped from training data, including dialogue speakers, who are replaced by stable UGF-vocabulary descriptors (Socrates → "the old teacher"; Demea → "the church man"; see `docs/followups/corpus-diversification.md`). This turns a second classical philosophy-of-language question into an empirical test, parallel to the inferentialism-of-predicates question that motivates this paper:

**Do the descriptors come to function as rigid designators?** Standard treatments distinguish singular-term content (Kripke 1972; Frege's *Bedeutung*) from predicate content. If the inferentialist program is right that conceptual content is constituted by inferential role — and that the same is true for *all* lexical items, including singular terms — then descriptors composed entirely of inferentially-active vocabulary should be able to develop the behavioral properties of names: rigidity across counterfactuals, substitution failure in opaque contexts, cross-context identity. If they cannot, then there is something distinctive about names that inferential role does not capture, and Brandom's program (which makes a determined effort to handle singular terms inferentially in *Making It Explicit* ch. 6) has a frontier the compression-v1 data have identified.

**The name-recovery experiment** (operationalized in `memory/names_rigid_designators.md`): after training, hold out canonical (original-name, descriptor) pairs and probe whether the Reasoner's representations support recovery of the original name from descriptor-in-context. High recovery = descriptor functions name-like even though the surface form was strictly predicational. Low recovery = the descriptor stayed predicational.

**Two pillars, one paper:**

1. *Inferentialism about predicates.* The prompt-attention deficit reveals a frontier (input-recognition) where inferential role alone does not fix the relevant cognitive capacity.
2. *Inferentialism about singular terms.* The descriptor experiment tests whether name-like behavior can emerge from purely inferential vocabulary.

Both pillars treat the compression-v1 setup as a clean empirical apparatus for testing inferentialism's reach. The paper becomes "two empirical purchase-points on inferentialism, one supporting and one constraining," which is both more interesting and more honest than a single thesis would be.

## What to write into paper G's Discussion

> **An inferentialist reading.** The prompt-attention deficit is the most original empirical finding, and it admits a philosophical reading. Inferentialism (Brandom 1994; Sellars 1956) holds that conceptual content is constituted by inferential role rather than by lexical label. If true, then a vocabulary compression that preserves inferential connections should preserve reasoning capacity — which is what we observe on within-distribution prompts. The deficit shows the limit: when the model is presented with prompts whose form differs from its training distribution, the inferential template runs but fails to engage the prompt's specific content. The inferential machinery is intact; the input-recognition layer is not. This pattern is what inferentialism predicts: inferential role is preserved under vocabulary compression, but the model's ability to recognize a prompt as triggering the relevant inferences is a separate, learnable capacity. We take this as a partial empirical vindication of inferentialism, with the prompt-attention question marking a frontier where the theory needs supplementation.
